A political fight broke out over speculation that a candidate defeated at the polls could return to the presidential office. The opposition called it 'revolving-door appointments,' while the ruling side countered that it is about using experience.
By Ju Yu-min · 2026.06.21

As the possibility of a defeated candidate returning to the presidential office was raised, sparring erupted between the parties—an old debate over the propriety of appointments resurfacing.

광고 문의 · 300×250

The opposition criticized reappointing a defeated figure to a key post as a 'revolving door' and as 'making light of the public,' arguing it improper to reappoint someone the ballot did not favor.

Differing views over appointments

Others counter that making use of a person's experience and expertise is part of normal governance, taking the view that election results and administrative ability should be judged separately.

The 'revolving door' controversy is a recurring theme across administrations. How to reconcile fairness in appointments with putting the right person in the right place becomes a point of contention each time.

Hwagyo Times holds that the legitimacy of an appointment is ultimately proven by governing results. More essential than the controversy itself is watching what results the appointee delivers in office.
